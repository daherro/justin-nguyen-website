I learned this the hard way, and I wish someone had told me before I tried.

There's a version of the product manager career path that used to make a lot of sense. You came out of consulting, you had sharp frameworks, you could run a room, you knew how to structure a problem and present it cleanly. That was enough. Product was a coordination role, and coordination was something consultants were trained to do.

I believed that version of the story. For a while, I think it was even true.

It isn't anymore.

## What I got wrong

When I came into product management from a consulting background, I assumed the core skill was synthesis — taking inputs from engineers, designers, and stakeholders and turning them into a coherent direction. And that *is* part of it. But I was treating synthesis as a substitute for understanding, and those are not the same thing.

What I didn't realize was how quickly your credibility erodes when you can't go under the hood.

Writing a PRD that your developers trust requires more than good formatting and clear acceptance criteria. It requires knowing what's actually hard. It requires understanding the architecture well enough to know when a feature that sounds simple is actually a six-week nightmare, and when something that sounds scary is a two-day config change. If you don't know the difference, you're not writing a roadmap — you're writing fiction with deadlines attached.

I dug myself into a deep hole because I had surface-level knowledge of how the product worked and I tried to operate as if that was enough. It wasn't. And the developers knew it before I did.

## The thing consultants aren't told

Consulting gives you a very specific kind of confidence. You've presented to C-suites, you've synthesized ambiguous problems under time pressure, you've managed up and across and sideways. You come into product feeling prepared.

But there's a skill that consulting doesn't train — and in some ways actively works against — which is the willingness to not know something, to sit with a codebase or a system diagram and admit you don't fully understand it yet. Consultants are rewarded for projecting command. That instinct will get you killed in a technical product role if it stops you from doing the work to actually learn the thing.

The consultants who are successfully making the transition into product right now are the ones who got real product exposure while they were still in consulting. Not just advising on product strategy. *Owning* something. Building something from zero to one for a client, being accountable for whether it shipped and whether it worked, learning to speak the language of the developers on the team because that was the only way to get things done.

If you have that, you can translate it. If you don't, the gap is bigger than most people realize.

## What "technical" actually means

I want to be precise here, because I think the word gets misused.

You don't have to be an engineer. You don't have to be able to write production code or debug a memory leak or design a schema from scratch. But you do have to be willing to be hands-on keyboard. You have to be willing to go into the system, look at how things are connected, understand the data model, follow the logic through. You have to be willing to look dumb in front of an engineer and ask a question that might have an obvious answer, because the alternative — pretending you understand and building decisions on top of that pretense — is so much worse.

The bar is: can you have a real conversation with the people building the product? Not a coordination conversation. A *technical* conversation where you understand what trade-offs are actually being made and why.

If you can do that, you can be an excellent product manager. If you can't, you'll always be operating one layer removed from the truth, and eventually that gap shows.

## The world that no longer exists

There was a moment — and I don't think it was that long ago — where strong general skills could carry you through a product role, especially at larger companies with structured teams and clear process. The role was more about alignment and communication than it was about depth.

That window has mostly closed. The companies worth working at, the roles worth having, they're looking for people who can do both. Who can communicate and align *and* go deep. The bar has moved, and it moved faster than most people coming out of consulting have caught up to.

This isn't a reason to despair. It's just a reason to be honest about what preparation actually looks like now.

## What I'd do differently

If I were advising someone coming out of consulting who wants to move into product, I'd tell them a few things.

First: while you're still in consulting, find a way to own something technical. Not just the strategy layer — the actual product. Build something, ship something, be accountable for whether it works. That experience is the difference between a resume that gets you in the door and one that gets you the offer.

Second: learn to read the systems. You don't have to become an engineer, but you should be able to look at an architecture diagram and understand what connects to what. You should be able to look at a data model and understand what the product is actually tracking. Spend time with the people who build the thing, and ask them to explain it to you until you actually get it.

Third: let go of the consulting reflex to project command before you have it. The best technical product managers I've seen are intensely curious and genuinely humble about what they don't know. That combination — curiosity plus humility — is what lets you close the gaps fast.

I'm still learning all of this. But I'm learning it because I had to, not because someone warned me in time.

Consider this the warning.
